Riverscape Community Genetics

Comparative population genetics of Ozark stream fishes

Background

Studies of genetic variation in natural populations typically focus on one species. Even studies aimed at comparing species rarely exceed 5-10 taxa.
The high-throughput capacity of next generation sequencing has made it possible to sequence many species in a single study. Analyses of such comprehensive data sets will be powerful to link processes happening at the micro-evolutionary level (e.g. gene flow) to patterns observed at the macro-evolutionary scale (e.g. speciation).

Objectives

The main objective of this work is to understand how dispersal affects population structure and ultimately diversification. A comparative approach of more than 30 species will be used to model the relationships between species specific traits linked with dispersal (e.g. morphology, life history, and niche breadth) and population structure. Significant traits gleaned from this modelling approach will then be used in tandem with a comparative phylogenetic framework to explore how these traits are correlated with diversification rates at a much broader temporal (i.e. evolutionary scale) and taxonomic scale (~ 1000 species).

Findings

Preliminary results obtained from population genetic analyses indicate much variability in population connectivity among species. Exploratory models suggest this variation is explained largely by ecological and life history differences among species and not necessarily morphological features, such as body size.

Landscape Genetics of White-tailed deer

Applying Genomics to Wildlife Epidemiology of Chronic Wasting Disease

I am working with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab in partnership with the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission to study the spread of Chronic Wasting disease.
Using state of the art genetic and computational tools we are predicting future spread of the disease. This study represents a genetic evaluation of 1,720 white-tailed deer sampled across 75 counties in Arkansas from 2016-2019. The genetic patterns discovered in our study reflect White-tail dispersal trajectories across landscapes over the course of generations, and help make predictions about the potential spread of CWD from the current Management Zone.

Fish Community Ecology

Exploring the factors that affect fish assemblage change in time and space

Background

I began my research as an MS student with Bill Matthews at the University of Oklahoma. There I lead a State Wildlife Grant funded project aimed at assessing the diversity in Oklahoma's southeastern most drainages (Muggy Boggy, Kiamichi, and Little River). This project resulted in rich data sets of fish assemblage and environmental characteristics for over 160 field sites. These data are being used to address questions related to the spatial and temporal dynamics of fish assemblages. (See publications)

Objectives

Numerous objectives exist regarding the original project and those that have evolved out of it. The first was to identify high diversity areas in the study region and categorize the taxonomic diversity there. Subsequent objectives have been developed including: 1) identifying major environmental drivers of fish assemblage turnover in the Muddy Boggy system; 2) comparing diversity and spatial dynamics among the three major drainages; 3) comparing contemporary diversity to that from 4 decades earlier to investigate the effect of spatial scale on estimating assemblage change; 4) quantifying probabilistic species associations and their stability through time; and 5) exploring the niche breadth of species of greatest conservation concern.

Findings

This work has demonstrated the importance of headwater stream systems for the maintenance of regional biodiversity. Although headwaters may be relatively depauperate at an individual location (i.e. alpha diversity), these streams have large species turnover among sites (i.e. beta diversity) and contribute significantly to overall drainage richness (i.e. gamma diversity). Furthermore, comparisons of change through time indicate the importance of spatial scale in estimating shifts in diversity and assemblage composition. While individual sites can differ dramatically through time due to stochasticity and fish movement, larger scales such as entire tributary systems remain relatively more stable through time. Therefore, spatial scale in paramount to understanding and responding to change in diversity of any organism.
