riverscape community genetics

comparative population genetics of stream fishes

Background

Patterns of genetic variation across the landscape can tell us a lot about the history of populations. For example, this is how companies like 23andMe trace human ancestry across the globe. We can do this for anything with genetic material to get an understanding of how populations have moved and changed through time. The goal of this project is to compare patterns of genetic variation of many species to learn more about how characteristics of the species themselves affect movement, population changes, and even speciation & extinction.

I have genotyped over 3,000 individual fish across 35 species throughout the White River Basin, USA.

Objectives

Some questions I seek to address with this work:

1) How frequent is hybridization in nature?

2) How does genetic population structure across the basin differ among species?

3) What role does the environment play in determining differences in genetic structure among species?

4) What role do traits play in determining differences in genetic structure among species?

5) Do traits related to genetic divergence affect processes at much larger time scales? (speciation and extinction)

Findings

Preliminary results indicate different species do show variability in how their genetic variation is structured across the riverscape, likely due to differences in population histories and connectivity via gene flow. Exploratory models suggest this pattern is explained largely by differences the species have in their ecological and life history traits and not necessarily morphological features, such as body size.

landscape genetics
of white-tailed deer

applying genomics to epidemiology

I am working with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab, in partnership with the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, to study the spread of Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) in White-tailed deer. CWD is caused by misfolded proteins in the brain (like Mad Cow Disease) that destroy the nervous system of infected individuals.

We use state-of-the-art genetic and computational tools to understand how deer have historically moved across the landscape of Arkansas, so that we can forecast disease spread. We have analyzed DNA of over 1,700 white-tailed deer across the state to infer longstanding patterns of gene flow mediated by deer dispersal. We then analyze these patters to determine environmental factors that either facilitate or mitigate deer movement to build predictive models of disease spread.

stream fish ecology

exploring the factors that affect fish assemblage change in time and space



Background

As a master’s student at the University of Oklahoma, working alongside Bill Matthews, I led a project funded by a state wildlife grant that aimed to assess diversity of fish species of greatest conservation need (SGCN) in the most specious part of Oklahoma. This project resulted in rich data sets of fish species occurrence and environmental characteristics at over 160 stream/river reaches. These data are being used to address questions related to how groups of fish, or assemblages, fluctuate in their composition across the riverscape and over time.

Objectives

The primary objective was to first identify high diversity areas as well as those that harbor high numbers of SGCN. Subsequent objectives have evolved including: 1) identifying major environmental drivers of fish assemblage structure; 2) comparing diversity and spatial dynamics among drainages; 3) comparing contemporary diversity to that from 40 years earlier and investigating the effect of spatial scale on estimating assemblage change; 4) identifying non-randomly associated species, inferring their likely causes, and exploring their stability through time.

Findings

This work has demonstrated the importance of headwater stream systems for the maintenance of regional biodiversity. Although headwaters may be relatively depauperate of species at an individual location (alpha diversity), these streams have large species turnover among sites (beta diversity) and contribute significantly to overall drainage richness (gamma diversity). Furthermore, comparisons of change through time indicate the importance of spatial scale in estimating shifts in diversity and assemblage composition. While individual sites can differ dramatically through time due to stochasticity and fish movement, larger scales such as entire tributary systems remain relatively more stable through time. Therefore, spatial scale is paramount to understanding and responding to change in diversity of any organism.

genetic diversity
of trans-himalayan fishes

conservation genomics

In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab, the National Research Center for Riverine and Lake Fisheries, the Ministry of Agricluture and Forests, and the Royal Government of Bhutan I have had the pleasure of visiting the "Land of the Thunder Dragon" and making expeditions to its streams and rivers in search of culturally important fish such as the Golden Mahseer.

The goal is to use genetics to understand how fish have both historically and contemporarily moved among the various tributaries across the Himalayas. This information is critical in the face of increasing development of hydroelectric dams in India and Bhutan. Importantly, our work also aims to build the fisheries research capacity in Bhutan.

conservation genomics
of a threatened fish

genetic diversity and connectivity

In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab and Illinois Natural History Survey I am assisting with research on the range-wide population genetics and phylogeography of the federally threatened Neosho Madtom.

The goal is to quantify spatial genetic structure, diversity, and connectivity of Neosho Madtom to assist with future conservation planning.

genetic diversity
of smallmouth bass

distinct lineages of the interior highlands

In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab and Arkansas Game and Fish Commission I am assisting with research on the regional population genetics and phylogeography of Smallmouth Bass.

The goal is to determine distinct lineages of the species across the interior highlands, where these lineages occur geographically, and where these lineages may be mixing. This information will help inform black bass management and help maintain evolutionarily significant units (ESUs) of diversity in the Ozarks.

population connectivity
of American black bear

managment of Arkansas bear

In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab I am assisting with research on the regional population genomics of Black Bear in Arkansas.

The goal is to use genomics (big data) to disentangle fine-scale population structure. This will allow us to estimate migration in and among the small populations of bear in the Ozarks.

fish diversity
in a contaminated drainage

Tar Creek Superfund site

Two world wars required raw matrials for ammunition provided by lead mines near Picher, OK. Unfortunately, groundwater was eventually contaminated due to these operations and eventually turned the surface water of Tar Creek into an orange disaster.

However, thanks to Robert Nairin and his team using environmental engineering to remediate parts of the watershed, the fish are returning to the once toxic waters. I helped sample Tar Creek and surrounding areas while at the University of Oklahoma with Bill Matthews. In parts of the watershed once completely devoid of fish, the communities now have recovered and harbor diversity similar to that in neighboring regions.
