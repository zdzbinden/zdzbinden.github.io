riverscape community genetics

comparative population genetics of stream fishes

Background

Patterns of genetic variation across the landscape can tell us a lot about the history of populations. This is how companies like “23andMe”, or “Ancestry” trace a given human’s ancestry across the globe, for example. We can do this for anything with genetic material to get an understanding of how populations have moved and changed through time. The goal of this project is to compare patterns of genetic variation across a region for many species to learn more about how characteristics of the species themselves affect movement, population changes, and even speciation & extinction.

Objectives

Some questions we seek to address with this work: 1) Do species differ in how genetic diversity is distributed across the landscape, and, if so, can species differences in characteristics explain this pattern? 2) Do species with low ability/desire to move across large distances (e.g. small or specialized species) more readily form sub-populations (i.e. “pockets” of unique genetic diversity distributed across the landscape)? 3) If mechanisms related to species characteristics are involved in creating contemporary patterns in genetic variability across the landscape (many generations required), then do these same characteristics play a role in the probability a given species divides into new and independent lineages (speciation) or faces population declines until total collapse (extinction)?

Findings

Preliminary results obtained from population genetic analyses indicate different species do show differences in how their genetic variation is structured across the landscape, likely due to differences in population histories and connectivity via gene flow. Exploratory models suggest this pattern is explained largely by differences the species have in their ecological and life history traits and not necessarily morphological features, such as body size.

landscape genetics
of white-tailed deer

applying genomics to epidemiology

I am working with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab, in partnership with the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, to study the spread of Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) in White-tailed deer. CWD is caused by misfolded proteins in the brain (like Mad Cow Disease) that destroy the nervous system of infected individuals.

We use state-of-the-art genetic and computational tools to understand how deer have historically moved across the landscape of Arkansas, so that we can forecast disease spread. We have analyzed DNA of over 1,700 white-tailed deer across the state to infer longstanding patterns of gene flow mediated by deer dispersal. We then analyze these patters to determine environmental factors that either facilitate or mitigate deer movement to build predictive models of disease spread.

stream fish ecology

exploring the factors that affect fish assemblage change in time and space



Background

As a master’s student at the University of Oklahoma, working alongside Bill Matthews, I led a state wildlife granted project aimed at assessing diversity of fish species of greatest conservation need (SGCN) in the most specious part of Oklahoma, its southeastern most drainages (Clear Boggy, Muggy Boggy, Kiamichi, and Little Rivers). This project resulted in rich data sets of fish assemblages and environmental characteristics for over 160 field sampling locations. These data are being used to address questions related to how groups of fish, or assemblages, fluctuate in their composition across the landscape and over time.

Objectives

The primary objective was first to identify high diversity areas across the region and categorize the taxonomic diversity there, with a particular focus on SGCN. Subsequent objectives have evolved including: 1) identifying major environmental drivers of fish assemblage spatial turnover; 2) comparing diversity and spatial dynamics among drainages; 3) comparing contemporary diversity to that from 40 years earlier and investigating the effect of spatial scale on estimating assemblage change; 4) identifying non-randomly associated species, inferring their likely causes, and exploring their stability through time; and 5) analyzing the niche breadth of species of greatest conservation concern compared to other species across the region.

Findings

This work has demonstrated the importance of headwater stream systems for the maintenance of regional biodiversity. Although headwaters may be relatively depauperate of species at an individual location (i.e. alpha diversity), these streams have large species turnover among sites (i.e. beta diversity) and contribute significantly to overall drainage richness (i.e. gamma diversity). Furthermore, comparisons of change through time indicate the importance of spatial scale in estimating shifts in diversity and assemblage composition. While individual sites can differ dramatically through time due to stochasticity and fish movement, larger scales such as entire tributary systems remain relatively more stable through time. Therefore, spatial scale is paramount to understanding and responding to change in diversity of any organism.
