riverscape community genomics

comparative population genomics of stream fishes

Background

Our ancestors were more likely to have kids with people nearby, e.g., from the same country/region. This resulted in people being more closely related (on average) to their countrymen/women, which means that today, we can actually infer the nationalities of our ancestors based on the DNA we carry. Many folks do not realize that other organisms, like fish, also have 'countries.' (Biologists call them populations.)

However, we are typically unaware of these 'countries' because other animals do not make maps or express their regional differences in a way we readily perceive. (Imagine fish with accents.) If we remain ignorant of these borders, then, at best, our management strategies will be ineffective and, at worst, detrimental because fish from different countries may have particular adaptations to their unique environments.

Therefore, to preserve the rich diversity across these 'countries' within a species, we must make maps to help guide conservation and management. We rely on information gathered from DNA samples collected across a species range to construct these maps. (Like 23andMe for fish!)

Objectives

But you might be thinking, "aren't many species out there, and wouldn't that take a lot of time and resources to make all those maps?"

Exactly! This project aims to understand what shapes fish countries by studying the maps of dozens of species along with multitudes of other data and approaches.

Suppose we can understand and model these general principles. In that case, we can make predictions about what maps for other species look like—even when we don't have any genetic data for them—and we can forecast how the countries might change in the future, for example, under various climate change scenarios.

Findings

Preliminary results indicate different species show variability in how their genetic variation is structured across the riverscape, likely due to differences in population histories and connectivity via gene flow. Exploratory models suggest this pattern is primarily explained by differences in species' ecological and life-history traits and not necessarily morphological features, such as body size.

    of white-tailed deer

    applying genomics to epidemiology

    I am working with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab and the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission to study the spread of Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) in White-tailed deer. CWD is caused by misfolded proteins in the brain (like Mad Cow Disease) that destroy the nervous system.

    We use state-of-the-art genetic and computational tools to understand how deer have historically moved across the landscape of Arkansas so that we can forecast disease spread. We analyze the DNA of deer across the state to infer longstanding patterns of gene flow mediated by deer dispersal. We analyze patterns to determine environmental factors that facilitate or mitigate deer movement to build predictive models of disease spread.

    stream fish ecology

    exploring the factors that affect fish assemblage change in time and space



    Background

    As a master’s student at the University of Oklahoma, working alongside Bill Matthews, I led a project funded by a state wildlife grant that aimed to assess the diversity of fish species of greatest conservation need (SGCN) in the most specious part of Oklahoma. This project resulted in rich data sets of fish species occurrence and environmental characteristics at over 160 stream/river reaches. These data are being used to address questions about how groups of fish, or assemblages, fluctuate in their composition across the riverscape and over time.

    Objectives

    The primary objective was first to identify high diversity areas and those that harbor high numbers of SGCN. Additional objectives have evolved, including 1) identifying major environmental drivers of fish assemblage structure; 2) comparing diversity and spatial dynamics among drainages; 3) comparing contemporary diversity to that from 40 years earlier and investigating the effect of spatial scale on estimating assemblage change; 4) identifying non-randomly associated species, inferring their likely causes, and exploring their stability through time.

    Findings

    Headwater streams are critical for the maintenance of regional biodiversity. Although headwaters may be relatively depauperate of species at an individual location (alpha diversity), these streams have large species turnover among sites (beta diversity) and contribute significantly to overall drainage richness (gamma diversity). Furthermore, comparisons of change through time indicate the importance of spatial scale in estimating shifts in diversity and assemblage composition. While individual sites can differ dramatically through time due to stochasticity and fish movement, larger scales such as entire catchments remain more stable. Therefore, spatial scale is paramount to understanding and responding to change in the diversity of any organism.

    genetic diversity
    of trans-himalayan fishes

    conservation genomics

    In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab, the National Research Center for Riverine and Lake Fisheries, the Ministry of Agriculture and Forests, and the Royal Government of Bhutan, I have had the pleasure of visiting the "Land of the Thunder Dragon" and making expeditions to its streams and rivers in search of culturally important fish such as the Golden Mahseer.

    The aim is to use genetics to understand how fish migrate among steep rivers that drain the Himalayas. This information is critical in the face of the increasing development of hydroelectric dams in India and Bhutan. Importantly, our work also aims to build the fisheries research capacity in Bhutan.

    conservation genomics
    of a threatened fish

    genetic diversity and connectivity

    In partnership with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab and Illinois Natural History Survey, I am studying the range-wide population genetics and phylogeography of the federally threatened Neosho Madtom.

    The goal is to quantify the spatial genetic structure, diversity, and connectivity of Neosho Madtom to assist with future conservation planning.

    genetic diversity
    of smallmouth bass

    distinct lineages of the interior highlands

    In collaboration with the Arkansas Conservation and Molecular Ecology Lab and Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, I am researching the regional population genetics and phylogeography of Smallmouth Bass.

    The study aims to determine distinct lineages of the species across the interior highlands, where these lineages occur geographically, and where these lineages may be mixing. This information will help inform black bass management and help maintain evolutionarily significant units (ESUs) of diversity in the Ozarks.

    fish diversity
    in a contaminated drainage

    Tar Creek Superfund site

    Two world wars required raw materials for ammunition provided by lead mines near Picher, OK. Unfortunately, groundwater was contaminated due to these operations and eventually turned the surface water of Tar Creek into an orange disaster.

    However, thanks to Robert Nairin and his team using environmental engineering to remediate parts of the watershed, the fish are returning to the once toxic waters. I sampled Tar Creek and surrounding areas while at the University of Oklahoma to assess re-establishment of fish populations. In parts of the watershed once utterly devoid of fish, the communities have recovered and harbor diversity similar to neighboring regions.
